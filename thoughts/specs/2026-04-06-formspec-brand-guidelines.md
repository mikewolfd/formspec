# Formspec Brand Guidelines

## Purpose

Formspec should feel like infrastructure with judgment.

It should not look bureaucratic, trendy, or generic. It should feel calm, exact, modern, and durable. The goal is not to make forms feel entertaining. The goal is to make consequential workflows feel legible, trustworthy, and well-shaped.

## Core Idea

Formspec is the language of structured intent.

A form is not a pile of controls. It is a workflow, a record, a contract, and often a moment of consequence. Formspec should present itself as the system that gives those moments structure without clutter, ambiguity, or unnecessary ceremony.

The brand should hold four qualities at once:

- human enough to feel considered
- precise enough to feel authoritative
- flexible enough to work across domains
- quiet enough not to compete with the task

## Positioning

Formspec should sit between:

- design systems that only style controls
- schema systems that only describe data
- low-code tools that flatten everything into the same UI
- developer tools that feel too technical for product teams

It should be framed as:

- a specification
- a runtime
- a shared language across design, product, engineering, and operations

It should not be framed as:

- a no-code toy
- a theme pack
- a visual gimmick
- a generic form library

Formspec should feel larger than a toolkit. It should feel like a standard.

## Personality

Formspec should feel:

- exact
- composed
- literate
- modern
- humane
- architected
- quietly confident

Formspec should not feel:

- cheerful for its own sake
- futuristic
- noisy
- over-decorated
- over-explained
- startup-generic

The right references are a blueprint, a field guide, a navigation system, or a well-edited technical publication. The wrong references are a dashboard marketing site, an enterprise portal, or a government template.

## Visual Direction

The visual world should communicate clarity without emptiness and structure without rigidity.

It should feel like:

- warm paper
- dark ink
- mineral green
- stone
- brushed metal
- technical notation
- editorial spacing

It should not feel like:

- neon product marketing
- glossy 3D software branding
- purple-on-white SaaS defaults
- default government blue
- abstract gradients with no real anchor

The visual standard is restrained, atmospheric, and materially grounded.

## Color

The palette should be disciplined and low-noise.

Default direction:

- warm neutrals for background and surface
- dark, slightly warm text
- one controlled accent color
- muted borders
- quiet status colors

Recommended roles:

- primary: deep mineral green
- surface: paper or parchment, not pure white
- text: near-black with warmth
- border: soft stone, never harsh gray
- error: disciplined red
- warning: ochre or amber
- info: muted blue, used sparingly

Rules:

- use one primary accent by default
- avoid multicolor systems unless the product genuinely needs them
- give backgrounds atmosphere without turning them into decoration
- keep contrast high and hierarchy obvious

## Typography

Typography should carry most of the identity.

The typographic voice should be:

- sharp
- compact
- editorial
- readable at length

Rules:

- the product name should be visually strong when brand is present
- headings should feel authored, not templated
- labels should be clear and calm, not heavy
- supporting copy should be short and precise
- branded surfaces should avoid a default system-stack feel

Typography should do more work than borders, shadows, or badges.

## Composition and Layout

Formspec layouts should feel architectural.

Principles:

- strong alignment
- visible section logic
- generous negative space
- few visual devices
- one idea per section
- hierarchy created through spacing and type before chrome

A Formspec surface should feel guided, not decorated.

Avoid:

- card soup
- stacked boxed panels
- icon clutter
- decorative separators
- repeated visual motifs
- full-width promotional action treatment in ordinary forms

## Form Experience

A Formspec form should communicate four things immediately:

- this system is structured
- this logic is intentional
- this workflow can be trusted
- your attention is being respected

That means:

- defaults should be calm
- validation should be clear, not alarming
- progression should feel guided, not gamified
- controls should feel tactile but not ornamental
- complex flows should become more legible as they deepen

The interface should never perform seriousness with more chrome than the task requires.

## Motion

Motion should reinforce structure and orientation.

Use motion for:

- conditional reveal
- workflow progress
- focus changes
- spatial transitions
- hierarchy

Motion should be:

- quick
- smooth
- restrained
- useful

Avoid:

- bounce
- flourish
- decorative hover noise
- celebratory transitions for routine actions

## Imagery

If Formspec uses imagery, it should support meaning rather than mood alone.

Prefer:

- documents in context
- real operational environments
- diagrams
- notation systems
- layered grids
- technical illustration

Avoid:

- smiling-team stock photography
- fake dashboard collages
- floating UI windows as the entire visual language
- generic gradient wallpaper

Formspec is better served by evidence, systems, and context than by hype imagery.

## Voice

The voice should be:

- concise
- exact
- grounded
- non-promotional
- slightly editorial

Good examples:

- Define form behavior once.
- Structure complex workflows without tying them to one UI.
- A specification for forms that need logic, validation, and long-term durability.
- Designed for serious workflows.

Bad examples:

- Build magical forms faster than ever.
- Supercharge your form experience.
- The ultimate AI-powered form revolution.

The brand should never sound like it is asking for attention.

## Messaging Order

When explaining Formspec, the message should usually move in this order:

1. structure
2. logic
3. durability
4. portability
5. presentation flexibility

It should not lead with speed, templates, or convenience.

## Product Surface Guidance

The default UI should express the brand by being:

- restrained
- elegant
- practical
- typographic
- compositional rather than decorative

In practice, that means:

- fewer loud full-width actions
- fewer boxed panels unless they serve containment
- softer borders
- warmer surfaces
- stronger section rhythm
- cleaner grouped choices
- workflows that feel guided rather than widgetized

## What Formspec Should Become

Formspec should become:

- the canonical way to describe serious digital forms
- a bridge between schema, design, logic, and execution
- a system that makes form-heavy products feel more intelligent
- an infrastructure brand with enough refinement to earn trust immediately

The aim is not to be the prettiest form library.

The aim is to be the most coherent system for building forms that matter.
